Data Mining  /  Introduction

A Data Mining Approach for Advice on Surviving the Videogame Industry

University of Colorado Boulder

The video game industry is a multi-billion dollar industry, and the evaluation continues to grow each year. Developments in technology have taken us from playing pong on the Atari system to virtual concerts inside of the wildly successful videogame Fortnite. Games such as the massive multiplayer online roleplaying games World of Warcraft and Guild Wars 2 allow players to play together and interact with each other in real-time when their geographical location might be thousands of miles apart. Additionally, continued development gave rise to Steam.com which is now the most popular distributor of PC/desktop games. While many of these games are developed by larger companies, the development of video game engines has led to a rise in independent video game development (or indie games).

The backgrounds of indie game developers varies widely. Some have worked in large professional studios, some have degrees ranging from computer science to game design, and some are gamers that are interested in making their own video games. Skill levels, knowledge levels, and experience vary widely. It follows that some indie developers know the process of building and shipping a full game with the potential of success while others are lost without a roadmap in an already cut-throat and competitive industry. While more and more game development resources are available on the internet in the forms of written instruction and YouTube.com video content, the backgrounds and expertise of the resource creators is often unknown.

In the case that the source of information is from a reputable or known source, the breadth of the information shared might be limited. Take the site Steamlytics.com and their entry for the widely popular indie game Hollow Knight. Steamlytics provides a sentiment analysis and a high level overview of the overarching response to the game. While the information could be helpful to know why Hollow Knight succeeded, it is difficult to learn any specifics. It would also be overly time consuming to analyze several games in this manner. Similar websites like gamalytic.com and vginsights.com provide high level information similar to Steamlytics, but the helpfulness suffers from the same lack of depth. Additionally, some information is behind a paywall, increasing the barrier of entry.

These limitations of current instructional content for video game development could be at least in part supplemented by an in-depth data-driven analysis of factors that may predict the success or failure of a new video game. Such data can be collected from Steam. Each game on Steam has associated images, trailers, music, reviews, popularity scores, etc. This data can provide insight into potential reasons for the success or failure of a given video game. It is reasonable to expect that through analyzing thousands of games, patterns will emerge in terms of the qualities that successful games share, that moderately successful games share, and that failed games share. These patterns could then be interpreted to inform guidelines and instructional content related to video game development.

For indie game developers, the benefit is clear. Analyzing patterns in game reviews, soundtracks, reviews, and screenshots can provide insight into shared key attributes among successful video games. This includes actual game development and design choices to marketing strategies involving screenshots and trailers. Additionally, better recommendation systems could obviously benefit a larger company like Valve by more efficiently connecting players with content that they enjoy but do not know exists.

Research Questions

  • 01 Do indie games tend to perform differently compared to AAA titles in terms of user feedback?
  • 02 To what extent can we predict a game's success based on promotional materials (description, screenshots, trailers)?
  • 03 How do game trailers impact user reviews and ratings?
  • 04 Do certain genres generally perform better in terms of user ratings?
  • 05 How do regional preferences shape the success of specific game genres?
  • 06 To what degree are the length of game trailers related to a game's user ratings?
  • 07 How does social media activity influence the success of a game?
  • 08 How do game reviews change over time post-release?
  • 09 Are there specific genres of music commonly found in successful games?
  • 10 What compositional elements in soundtracks are correlated with positive user reviews?

Data Collection and Exploration

Data Collection

The data utilized in this work comes directly from Steam via an API. There are three main API calls utilized to collect the data:

https://api.steampowered.com/ISteamApps/GetAppList/v2/
→ Acquire all Steam app IDs
https://store.steampowered.com/api/appdetails?appids=<APPID>
→ Collect details about a single Steam application
https://store.steampowered.com/appreviews/<APPID>?json=1&num_per_page=100&cursor=<CUR>&filter=recent&purchase_type=all
→ Paginated review collection

Data Cleaning

Formatting the data into CSV files in this manner effectively cleans every column except for the description column. In that column, HTML tags are present as well as other non-text characters. The HTML tags are filtered out, and the description for any game that does not provide a coherent description is set to an empty string. Removing entire rows is avoided because of the other useful information and since not all analyses are dependent on the description.

The descriptions and reviews are transformed into transaction format for later analysis as another stage of cleaning.

Data Mining  /  Models Implemented

Models

In order to model the data, we took a few different approaches and utilized a combination of unsupervised and supervised methods.

Unsupervised

Association Rule Mining

Model Explanation

We applied association rule mining to the reviews of the games. We looked at Steam Apps classified as a "game" and examined differences among the top 10 genres. We filtered game reviews by the ratio of positive reviews to negative reviews. If a game had more positive reviews than negative, it was considered a positive game and vice versa.

ARM requires transaction data. To create it, we extracted reviews by liked/disliked classification and genre, then removed line break characters, capital letters, punctuation, numbers, non-latin characters, duplicate words, and stopwords. Multimodal

CLIP

Model Explanation

CLIP is a multimodal model proposed by OpenAI that aligns textual and image embeddings into a joint space. It was trained to predict which caption fits best for an image among candidates, allowing it to learn associations of visual and textual data. Trained on diverse image-text pairs, CLIP can execute tasks such as image classification, retrieval, and zero-shot learning without fine-tuning for individual tasks. Using a dual encoder architecture, it processes images through a vision model (ResNet or ViT) and processes text using a transformer to produce embeddings to be compared.

Why We Used It

We utilized the CLIP model to examine and find associations between game images and their textual descriptions. The ability of this model to create a common embedding space for text and images allowed us to efficiently perform tasks like image classification and retrieval, helping uncover patterns and improve insight into relationships between visuals and game descriptions.

Data Cleaning

The images below show data before (top) and after (bottom) cleaning. This cleans game descriptions of noise like irrelevant words and characters, making the text data concise and meaningful. The preprocessing step improves the quality of textual embeddings generated by CLIP, improving its ability to relate game descriptions with corresponding images. Cleaning was also done for the image dataset, such as removing 404 error URLs and filtering to screenshots only for more consistent training.

Model Evaluation (Using Similarity Scores)

The similarity scores indicate how well text embeddings align with image embeddings in the CLIP model. Each value represents the similarity between a text-image pair where higher scores indicate stronger associations. Scores are cosine similarity between 0 and 1. Since we are finding custom descriptions for completely new images, even a score above 0.2 is considered good.

The CLIP model is very effective in describing what each game image is. In a matrix of 10 images, it was able to correctly describe each image. The examples of results provided show the capability of CLIP in analyzing and describing game screenshots in high detail. For each game image, CLIP identified relevant tags or genres such as "space combat simulation," "stealth game," "resource management game," and "visual novel," showcasing its capability to understand and interpret visual data in the gaming context.

What We Learned

This functionality has many applications in the analysis of games. CLIP enables automation in categorizing and tagging games with screenshots, reducing human effort and improving scalability for large gaming datasets. This can be useful for game developers and platforms in streamlining metadata creation, making it easier for end-users to discover or search for games. It also improves the quality of recommendation systems by allowing them to suggest games with appealing visuals or gameplay themes that appeal to a user's preferences.

Supervised

Gradient Boosting

Model Explanation

Gradient Boosting is a powerful machine learning technique that generates models sequentially, each new model correcting errors of the previous ones. A weak learner (usually decision trees) is combined into a strong predictive model through minimizing a loss function. Gradient Boosting has a wide range of applications in regression and classification problems, where it maintains accuracy and flexibility.

Why We Used It

We applied Gradient Boosting to the image dataset for effective modeling of dependencies between features including brightness, saturation, hue, and contrast. Its ability to deal with non-linear patterns and emphasize misclassified examples ensures accurate predictions. The dataset was created from image URLs, containing app IDs and image feature values such as brightness, contrast, saturation, and hue, to find patterns in underlying image aspects and game success.

Model Evaluation (Using Mean Squared Error)

We use Mean Squared Error to calculate an average of the squared difference between predicted and actual values. The lower the MSE, the better the accuracy of the model. At a value of 130, the model performance is good, showing that predictions are relatively near to actual values, indicating effective learning.

Output

The model predicts the success rate, which is the ratio of number of positive reviews to number of negative reviews. It rates higher success rates to games with more interesting game image screenshots and lesser success rates to dull or confusing game image screenshots.

Correlation heatmap showing feature relationships. Most features are unrelated except contrast and brightness, which show some correlation.
Relationship between image saturation and review ratio. Higher saturation tends to correlate with lower success rates.
A contrast value between 50 and 80 appears to be the sweet spot for higher success rates. Too low or too high contrast both correlate with reduced success.
Boxplot showing hue vs. success rate. All hue ranges show similar success rates; hues between 101-127 (green-yellow) are slightly higher, but no strong conclusion can be drawn.
No clear relationship exists between image brightness and game success rate.

What We Learned

From this model, we extracted a few important points about how image features such as brightness, contrast, and saturation reflect success rates of games through reviews. The model predicted an optimal range of 50 to 80 for contrast for high success rates. Saturation is negatively correlated with the success rate, which might indicate that highly saturated images could turn players off. Hue did not correlate significantly with success, indicating consumer preference is not strongly connected with specific colors. No obvious influence of brightness could be found on the success rate. The model performs well with an MSE of 130, showing effective learning of the relationship between image features and game success.

Data Mining  /  Conclusions and Results

Conclusions

Video games are at their core interactive mediums for art. They bring together elements of narrative design, art, music, sound effects, and more.

Results showed that a quality story was one of the most appreciated aspects of video games, and it held a central point in analyses. This makes sense on multiple levels. Art is largely about telling stories. Songs tell stories to a listener. Theatrical plays and movies show a story acted out. Books create entire worlds using a reader's imagination. Humans naturally seek out stories and are drawn to them.

This raises a question of whether or not stories are more important than the other elements involved. Do art, music, sound, and game mechanics take a back seat? We say no. Without these foundational elements, a story has no support. Take the massively multiplayer genre, of which games like World of Warcraft and Guild Wars 2 are members. The central word tied to all others in negative reviews was "can't" — obviously a negative word in context, indicating poor massively multiplayer games were plagued by the player's inability to do something. This would serve as an example of limited player agency or independence.

A common idea in video game development is that audience attention should not be fully drawn to any individual supporting element. If it is, that is probably a clear sign that something is off. These are often supporting elements adding to a whole. Too much attention drawn to any individual element is distracting from the bigger picture.

Art and Image Analysis

In the realm of art, we showed the potential to translate a static image into a short game description. Art is almost always a game's first impression, and many developers and players have anecdotally stated that art can make or break a game's success. The ability to create a static image and have it automatically labeled gives a game developer, character artist, or environmental artist a strong tool to seek out games with similar art styles and be inspired by the genres of these games.

Additionally in the realm of art, we show a potential link between elements of game art (like saturation and contrast) with game success. Results showed potential optimal levels of contrast and saturation in images. A mid-range contrast amount predicted slightly better performance, and higher levels of saturation tended to be indicative of worse performing games.

Future Work

This project represents a small part of the potential future analyses that could be completed. Reviews and art represent only a portion of the video game landscape. In the future, further work could be dedicated to:

  • Integrating more types of data together (metadata, audio, video analysis)
  • Music and sound effect analysis for patterns in successful game soundtracks
  • Lighting effects and visual design pattern analysis
  • Story design and narrative structure analysis
  • Interaction design analysis and player agency metrics
  • Market analytics and platform-specific success patterns
